# World & Lore Bible

## The Event

No one knows what happened. One morning, everything changed.

All technology stopped working simultaneously — phones, electricity, engines, networks, all of it. Gone overnight. But that wasn't the strangest part.

The strangest part was the powers.

People woke up able to conjure fire, freeze water mid-air, call down lightning from a clear sky. Not everyone — but enough. Ordinary people: teachers, teenagers, mechanics, retirees. Most of them frightened. Some of them not.

The cause remains unknown. No warning. No explanation. No one came to explain it. The world just... changed.

## Tone & Atmosphere

This is **not** a classic fantasy setting. Magic is not ancient, learned, or noble here. It did not exist yesterday. The people wielding it did not study in towers or inherit power from bloodlines — they woke up with it and had to figure it out alone.

The emotional register should feel like:
- Dislocation. Power that doesn't feel like it belongs to you.
- Improvisation. People adapting to something they never asked for.
- Low-grade dread. The mystery of *why* is always present.
- Occasional dark humor. People are people even when the world breaks.

**Avoid:**
- High-fantasy clichés: "arcane", "ancient power", "the prophecy", "chosen ones"
- Magic as noble or elegant — it can be brutal, strange, uncontrolled
- Lore that implies magic was always part of this world

**Lean into:**
- Ordinary names for extraordinary things (people name their spells like they named their pets — casually, personally)
- The sense that civilization is patched together from what survived
- Enemies who are also just people/creatures who got powers — not monsters from a mythology

## The World Now

- Technology is gone. Civilization continues in reduced, makeshift form.
- Wizards (the common term for power-havers, though many dislike the word) are a small but significant part of the population.
- There is no governing body for magic. No schools. No guilds (yet). People learn from each other, from trial and error, from danger.
- The wilderness is more dangerous than before — creatures were affected too.

## Wizard Classes — Flavor Notes

Each class isn't a formal title — it's shorthand for what power someone manifested. They're more like descriptors than ranks.

| Class | What Happened to Them |
|---|---|
| Pyromancer | Woke up setting things on fire. Learned to aim. |
| Tidecaller | Water responds to them. Useful. Also terrifying. |
| Stonewarden | The earth listens. Slow to act, hard to stop. |
| Stormseeker | Gets static shocks constantly. Learned to redirect it. |
| Frostweaver | Everything they touch gets cold. Has its advantages. |
| Shadowblade | Their shadow does things it shouldn't. Drains to heal. |
| Dawnmage | Light bends for them. Protective. Calm. Hard to read. |
| Verdantmaker | Plants respond. Vines move. Roots obey. Unsettling. |

## Naming Conventions

Keep names grounded and functional, not mythic. Spells are named by what they do, not what legend says about them.

- Good: *Ember Shot, Rock Throw, Dark Pact, Healing Rain*
- Avoid: *Flames of Eternity, The Warden's Ancient Curse, Rite of the Frozen Tomb*

Enemy names follow the same logic — they describe what the creature is, not its place in a mythology.

## Open Questions (Intentionally Unresolved)

- What caused The Event?
- Why did only some people get powers?
- Why did technology stop?
- Are more changes coming?
- Is there a pattern to who got which type of power?

These questions are **not answered in-game**. They are the texture of the world — present in the atmosphere, never resolved.
